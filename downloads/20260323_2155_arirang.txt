1. 
Global oil prices are surging amid escalating tensions in the Middle East, putting South Korea on high alert over potential supply disruptions.

Uncertainty around the Strait of Hormuz — which carries about 20 percent of global oil — has driven sharp price swings after Donald Trump demanded that Iran reopen the route.
According to Bloomberg on Monday, Brent crude briefly climbed above 114 dollars a barrel, while the U.S. benchmark WTI crossed the 100-dollar mark.

The instability comes after the International Energy Agency warned that the crisis could rival or exceed past global energy shocks. Speaking via AFP, the IEA's chief said the situation could combine the impact of the 1970s oil shocks and the 2022 energy crisis, citing widespread damage to energy infrastructure across the region.
 
2.
Seoul has called on Tehran to halt attacks on civilians and civilian facilities in Gulf countries,... and to ensure safe navigation through the Strait of Hormuz.
Foreign Minister Cho Hyun said this in a phone call with his Iranian counterpart Seyyed Abbas Araghchi {세예드 압바스 아락치} on Monday evening.

South Korea's foreign ministry says Cho expressed deep concern that the worsening Middle East situation is affecting not only the region,... but also global security and economy,... asking Iran to stop targeting civilians and public infrastructure,... and to help restore normal global energy supplies.

Cho also asked for Iran’s special attention and support for the safety of South Korean nationals in the country.

He noted that many vessels,... including South Korean ships,... are currently anchored in the Strait of Hormuz,... and requested necessary safety measures from the Iranian side.

Seoul says Araghchi explained Tehran’s position,... and the two sides agreed to stay in communication.
 
3.
President Lee Jae Myung presided over the first Central Integrated Defense Council meeting of his administration at the Blue House on Monday.
The council convenes officials from the government, the military, and the civilian sector to assess the country's defense readiness and review key policy outcomes.

There, President Lee described self-reliant defense as the key to South Korea's defense system, citing an "increasingly complex and rapidly changing geopolitical environment."

He called for strengthening preparedness so the country can defend itself under any circumstances, while expressing confidence in South Korea's capabilities.

 
4.
The South Korean won closed at 1,517.3 against the U.S. dollar on Monday, finishing above the 1,510 won mark for the first time in 17 years. 

This depreciation stems from extreme volatility gripping global financial markets.
Following Iran's blockade of the Strait of Hormuz and a 48-hour ultimatum issued by U.S. President Donald Trump, international oil prices have surged,... leading to investors turning to safe-haven assets.
In fact, the dollar index climbed back toward the 100 mark.

The benchmark KOSPI also took a hit, closing at 5,405.75... down 6.49 percent from the previous closing.
The steep decline prompted authorities to activate a sell-sidecar early in the session to temporarily halt program trading. Foreign investors led the exodus, offloading trillions of won in local shares. 
 
5.
North Korea has re-appointed Kim Jong-un as chairman of the State Affairs Commission,... in a leadership reshuffle that indicates tighter regime control.

The North's state media said Monday that the Supreme People’s Assembly,... the regime’s rubber-stamp parliament,... held the first meeting of its 15th term on Sunday.

Kim was once again elevated to the top state post,... while one of his closest aides, Jo Yong-won,... replaced Choe Ryong-hae as president of the Presidium of the Supreme People’s Assembly,... a position equivalent to parliamentary speaker.

Jo was also named first vice chairman of the State Affairs Commission,... seemingly sealing his status as one of the most powerful figures in the regime.
At the same time, Kim Jong-un's younger sister Kim Yo-jong was removed from the State Affairs Commission.
