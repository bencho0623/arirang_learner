1.
As the conflict in the Middle East nears the one-month mark, 
concerns are growing in South Korea over possible energy shortages.
President Lee Jae Myung today called for energy saving 
during an emergency economic meeting.
President Lee chaired an emergency economic meeting with relevant ministries at the Blue House on Thursday, the second such meeting 
since the conflict began last month.
The meeting came a day after the government announced the launch 
of an emergency economic task force led by Prime Minister Kim Min-seok.
In his opening remarks, Lee delivered a direct message to the public, 
urging them to help conserve energy, particularly electricity.
The Korea Electric Power Corporation is the state-run utility 
responsible for supplying nearly all of the country's electricity.
Its debt has surpassed 2-hundred-trillion Korean won, 
which is more than 1-hundred-32-billion U.S. dollars.

2.
Two weeks after implementing a domestic oil price cap, 
the government has set new two-week price caps for gasoline, diesel, 
and household kerosene, saying they will be raised slightly on Friday 
in response to sharply rising international oil prices.
The gasoline price ceiling will rise from the current 
1-thousand-7-hundred-24 won to 1-thousand-9-hundred-34 won per liter, which is around 1.29 U.S. dollars, while diesel will increase from 
1-thousand-7-hundred-13 won to 1-thousand-9-hundred-23 won.
Household kerosene will also go up from 1,320 won to 1,530 won per liter.
Compared to the price caps set two weeks ago, these adjustments represent a uniform increase of 210 won across the board. 

3.
The government and the ruling Democratic Party are pushing for a supplementary budget proposal, set to be submitted to the 
National Assembly on March 31st for swift processing.
The announcement was made Thursday morning during a 
consultative meeting between government officials and DP lawmakers.
This is part of efforts to mitigate the economic impact 
of the prolonged war in the Middle East.
The plan is aimed at easing the burden of rising fuel costs, stabilizing livelihoods, and supporting small and mid-sized firms.

4.
South Korea’s economic growth forecast for this year 
has been downgraded by the OECD.
According to the OECD's March Interim Economic Outlook on Thursday, South Korea's economic growth for 2026 is now projected at 1.7 percent.
That is a 0.4 percentage point drop from the organization's 
previous forecast in December.
Inflation in South Korea is also expected to worsen, 
with the forecast adjusted to 2.7 percent, up 0.9 percentage points.
The primary driver behind this gloomy outlook is South Korea's structural vulnerability to global energy shocks.
As a manufacturing powerhouse that imports nearly all of its crude oil
with a massive share coming directly from the Middle East, 
prolonged warfare poses a direct threat to domestic production.

BTS's "SWIM," the lead single off its fifth album "Arirang," 
recorded roughly twice as many streams as the band's hit "Dynamite" 
on its first day on Spotify, reaching 14.64 million.
This figure is 1.3 times and 1.9 times more than BTS's previous hits 
"Butter" and "Dynamite."
Following their comeback show at Gwanghwamun Square, BTS saw first-time listeners on Spotify surge by over 690%, while streams of their new album jumped 120% after their performance.
The group also appeared on "The Tonight Show Starring Jimmy Fallon" 
on Wednesday, delivering the first-ever TV performance of "SWIM" 
at the Guggenheim Museum, and are set to return for a 
second consecutive appearance on Thursday.
