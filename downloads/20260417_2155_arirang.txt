The Ministry of Oceans and Fisheries said a South Korean tanker unable to pass through the Strait of Hormuz safely reached Saudi Arabia via the Red Sea, loaded crude oil there, and has now departed for South Korea.

This is the first time South Korea has brought crude oil home via the Red Sea since the Strait of Hormuz was shut, opening a new supply route through one of the world's dangerous waterways.
The Red Sea remains a high-risk area because it is a base of operation for Yemen's Iranian-backed Houthi rebels.
Since violence broke out between Israel and Hamas in October 2023, there have been 79 attacks on merchant vessels in the region.

The ministry said it monitored the vessel around the clock as it sailed through the Red Sea, providing real-time information and maintaining constant communication to help ensure the safety of the crew.

President Lee said in a post on social media platform X on Friday that he had received what he called "welcome news" that a South Korean vessel was safely transporting crude oil through the Red Sea.
The president said the operation was a valuable achievement made possible by government agencies working together as one team.
He also thanked everyone involved, especially the crew, for their efforts under difficult conditions.
Lee said the government is mobilizing all available resources and will remain fully prepared to protect public safety and the national interest.

2.
South Korea has welcomed a newly announced ceasefire between Israel and Lebanon, calling it a key step toward easing tensions in the Middle East.

In a statement on Friday, Foreign Ministry spokesperson Park Il said Seoul fully supports the 10-day truce, and praised U.S. President Donald Trump’s efforts in helping bring it about.
Seoul urged all parties to strictly adhere to the agreement, and to respect Lebanon’s sovereignty and territorial integrity.

The statement also expressed hope the ceasefire will help advance U.S.–Iran negotiations, restore regional stability, and allow for the safe and free passage of vessels through the Strait of Hormuz.

President Lee Jae Myung will join a virtual summit led by the UK and France on Friday, Korea Standard Time, according to the Blue House.
The initiative, co-chaired by French President Emmanuel Macron and British Prime Minister Keir Starmer, seeks a multilateral solution to secure passage through the Strait of Hormuz, one of the world's most vital shipping lanes.

Around 70 to 80 participants, including countries and international organizations, have been invited.

A senior presidential official said that Seoul decided to join the talks, citing both national and global interests.
The official added that President Lee may deliver remarks during the meeting, and staff were "preparing for this possibility."

The meeting's agenda covers a broad range of issues, with a heavy focus on energy supply chains and the broader conflict in the Middle East.
However, the official stressed that this international mission will be strictly defensive in nature.

As the U.S.-Iran war stretches beyond six weeks, fuel surcharges have jumped to record highs,... rising multiple times compared to before the war.

According to the aviation industry, the benchmark used to set next month's fuel surcharge... the average price of Singapore jet fuel from March 16th to April 15th... rose to about 5 U.S. dollars and ten cents per gallon.

That pushes the surcharge to the highest bracket, level 33.
The surcharge stood at level six before the war, but in the space of just two months it surged past level 18 to reach 33... marking the fastest increase on record.
This surpasses the previous peak of level 22 seen during the Russia-Ukraine war.
Pressure on the airline industry is building quickly.

Fuel typically accounts for about 30 percent of an airline's operating costs, so a surge in jet fuel prices is bound to hit earnings hard.

According to an industry official, fuel surcharges cannot be raised freely, as they are capped under a tiered system.
