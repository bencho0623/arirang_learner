President Lee Jae Myung says South Korea must maintain its emergency response system following the breakdown of peace talks between the United States and Iran.

Chairing a Cabinet meeting on Tuesday, Lee warned that high oil prices and disruptions to global energy and raw material supplies are likely to persist.

He called for the swift implementation of the roughly 17 billion U.S. dollar supplementary budget to support people's livelihoods.
The government plans to begin distributing cash handouts to the bottom 70 percent of income earners starting April 27th.
Lee also urged the parties to the Middle East conflict to take what he called a "brave step" toward peace, based on "universal human rights and the lessons of history."

3.
As the Strait of Hormuz has remained closed for more than a month, oil-producing countries in the Middle East are looking to South Korea as a potential oil storage hub.

Speaking at a briefing in Sejong on Tuesday, Yang Ki-wook, director general of the office of Industry, Trade and Resource Security at the Ministry of Trade, Industry and Resources, said Middle Eastern countries want to use Korea's petroleum storage sites.

Director General Yang said exporters are trying to reduce their dependence on the Strait of Hormuz, explaining that countries such as Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Kuwait could face even more economic damage than South Korea if the strait remains closed, as crude oil exports account for a major share of their economies.
He said those countries believe they can lower the risk by storing oil outside the strait, then selling it later if needed.
Yang added that several governments have already asked South Korea to begin talks on using oil storage facilities.

Under South Korea's joint oil storage project, foreign oil companies can store crude in facilities run by the Korea National Oil Corporation.
In return, Seoul earns rental fees and secures the right of first refusal, allowing the government to purchase the oil first during a supply emergency.
 
4.
The U.S. and Iran could return to the negotiating table later this week, after their first round of talks ended over the weekend without a breakthrough.
According to Reuters on Tuesday, multiple sources say the two sides may resume negotiations in the Pakistani capital, Islamabad, where the initial talks were held.

While an exact date has not been set, an Iranian official said they are keeping their schedules open between the 17th and 19th, while a U.S. official told AP that talks could take place as early as the 16th.
Pakistani officials, who are mediating between the two sides, say they are in contact with both governments and that another round of talks is likely to take place over the weekend.

According to the Wall Street Journal, Washington has proposed a framework of up to 20 years, while the New York Times reports that Tehran has put forward a counterproposal with a shorter timeline of around five years.

The report adds that the U.S. is also offering possible sanctions relief in return, marking a shift from its previous demand for a complete halt to enrichment.
 
5.
Beyondparallel, an analytic vehicle focused on Korean unification, reported on Monday that a building at Yongbyon, believed to be a new uranium enrichment plant, seems to have been completed.
Last June, the International Atomic Energy Agency(IAEA) said North Korea was building a uranium enrichment plant to produce weapons-grade material for nuclear weapons.
Satellite imagery from early April showed the completion of various facilities, such as a backup generator, administrative building, and garage, and people currently working on internal construction.

Meanwhile, another satellite image of Pyongsan, Hwanghaebuk-do Province, shows signs that suggest North Korea is expanding its nuclear weapons production.
The construction of the roof, which is a noticeable blue color, indicated that work has been completed.

In particular, a facility processing uranium ore into “uranium concentrate,” a key raw material for nuclear weapons, appears to be operating.
