#1
Marking BTS’s return, fans are pouring in from overseas, fueling a sharp rise in arrivals to South Korea in recent weeks.
As BTS gears up for a long-awaited performance in central Seoul on Saturday, fans from around the world are already arriving, eager to witness what many are calling "historic."
Foreign arrivals to South Korea have surged by more than thirty percent this March from a year earlier.
According to data from the Ministry of Justice, nearly 1-point-1 million foreigners entered the country between March 1st and 18th up roughly 32-point-7 percent on-year.
Arrivals in these groups jumped 40 percent and 35-point-2 percent respectively, exceeding the overall growth rate.

#2
Authorities in South Korea carried out a high-level, on-site safety inspection Friday, just one day ahead of BTS’s highly anticipated full-group comeback performance at Gwanghwamun Square.
The inspection, led by the interior and safety minister alongside officials from Seoul city, police, fire authorities, and HYBE, reviewed crowd control, emergency response systems, and potential risk factors along the entire event route – from subway access points to the main stage and nearby rooftops.
The concert, set for March 21st, is expected to draw up to 260 thousand people, marking the first time since the 2002 and 2006 World Cup celebrations that more than 200 thousand attendees will gather in the Gwanghwamun area for a cultural event. With those numbers in mind, authorities are rolling out what they describe as an unprecedented, stadium-level crowd management plan. 
Around 6,500 police officers will be deployed, along with some 800 emergency personnel and over 100 pieces of rescue equipment, including ambulances.
Transportation will also be heavily impacted.
Major roads around Sejong-daero will be closed starting the night before the event, while nearby subway stations — including Gwanghwamun, Gyeongbokgung, and City Hall — will be bypassed during peak hours on the day of the concert.

#3
The war in the Middle East has now entered its third week.
Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu held his second press conference since the outbreak of the war, where he said that Iran has effectively lost its capacity to enrich nuclear fuel and manufacture ballistic missiles.
He added that signs of cracking are emerging within Iran’s leadership, signaling the possibility that the conflict could end sooner than many expect.
Netanyahu also said that Israel “acted alone” in attacking Iranian infrastructure related to the South Pars gas field.
He also dismissed claims that Israel sought to draw the U.S. into the conflict as “fake news.”

#4
The Blue House says it is in close communication with key partners, including the United States, on how South Korea can "contribute to the situation in the Strait of Hormuz."
A Blue House official told Arirang News on Friday that Seoul hopes global maritime logistics will return to normal as soon as possible, acknowledging that the safety of international shipping lanes and the freedom of navigation serve the interests of all countries. 
The same official added that the government is carefully reviewing its response options, taking into account domestic laws and procedures, as well as its defense posture on the Korean Peninsula

#5
Kim Jong-un has claimed North Korea’s new battle tank has "unmatched" capabilities, as he attended a drill that showcased his push for high-tech weaponry, while again placing his teenage daughter into the spotlight.
The North's Korean Central News Agency reported Friday that Kim oversaw a joint offensive tactical drill by infantry and tank sub-units, at a military training base in Pyongyang.
At the center of the exercise was a new tank equipped with an active protection system, which the North says can automatically detect and intercept incoming anti-tank missiles and drones.
State media claimed the system showed a 100 percent hit rate, striking threats approaching from multiple directions.
